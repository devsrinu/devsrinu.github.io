Get to know how authentication is evolving

Get to know about the latest happenings in the authentication world

February 21, 2020 Srini

5 minute read

How was your login today?

“Software is eating the world”. That’s really true these days. Can you imagine not having your phone for a day? Also true: You need to login to do any meaningful work.

Also true: Your data is very valuable than ever.

Also true: Most of the websites, apps provide poor login/authentication experience.

Why is it so?

It is for a variety of reasons:

  • Authentication is not the core experience of most businesses, but every app needs to authenticate the users.

  • Most businesses might not be even thinking about providing a secure authentication experience (They would do when bad things happen but it would be too late)

  • It’s not easy to build, implement, deploy and maintain an authentication service that is secure yet easy to use

  • Traditionally businesses had to buy bloated, expensive on premise software from traditional vendors and those solutions do not provide lot of customization and hence they end up with offering an authentication experience that is not very modern and easy

So what has changed in the last few years?

There are variety of developments happening in the authentication area that are trying to make it easier to login and yet provide better security and privacy.

I wanted to touch on few of those as you evaluate your login experience across apps, websites.

Password

Password has been one of the popular mechanisms to authenticate and as you know it also makes our life very difficult. Check if the service allows to login without having to use password. Caution: Some websites offer google, facebook login to authenticate but beware that it usually means you make it easier for them to track you.

Email

As the complexity of managing passwords grows, some services are trying to use your email account as a way to authenticate. This typically involves the website sending a link to your email and then you click the link to login.

This is little better than using password for every app but it just delegates all your password problems to the email account service as you still need a password to log into your email account.

Phone (SMS, Call)

This one seems very convenient but could be very risky for the following few reasons:

  1. You are sharing your phone number with the service (privacy alert)

  2. You risk takeover of your phone account depending on the value of prize (your bitcoin/bank account). There are several high profile stories about this kind of attack.

  3. Attackers can trick you into revealing the code that is sent to your phone. There are several real life cases of social engineering that result in fraudulent use of your account.

Authenticator apps

Examples of these are: Google Authenticator, Authy, Duo, Okta Verify.

In case you are not familiar, the idea behind the implementation of these is to have “shared” secret key. A copy of it is stored on your phone and the other copy is on the server that serves the content of your app/website.

There is a standard algorithm called TOTP that generates 6 digit number based on the current time and the secret key.

Your website/app asks for this code and you copy the code from authenticator app to login.

I like this for the following reasons:

  1. There is no transmission of information from the server to the authenticator app to generate this code, so there is no risk of interception.

  2. There is no need to share your phone number while enrolling into the authenticator app.

  3. The authenticator app doesn’t require internet connectivity so this works well when you don’t have good phone signal.

Beware of the following:

  1. Attackers can still trick you into revealing the code via social engineering attacks or phishing.

  2. Don’t use random/suspicious authenticator apps since they could compromise the shared secret key. So use authenticator apps from reputed companies which are really in the business of authentication.

Some of these provide advanced capabilities like authenticating using a push notification instead of you copying the code.

WebAuthn (FIDO)

This is an emerging web standard and almost all popular browsers started supporting it.

I am very excited about this one in particular because of its focus on privacy, protection against phishing and strong security (uses public key cryptography).

Most of the times the products that implement this standard also leverage biometrics without having to share your data with the website/app. In that sense all of your biometrics are on your device and never shared with anyone.

Following products implement this standard:

  • Yubikey (security key) from Yubico

  • Windows Hello (available on Windows 10)

  • Macbook TouchID

Apple also has recently joined the FIDO alliance as well in support of this standard.

I have heard great things from people who use WebAuthn in terms of ease of use and security. I expect that things would get only better as browsers provide support for this standard.

Hardware tokens

Most of the hardware tokens are based on shared secret (similar to authenticator apps). In that sense the only difference is that the form factor is hardware instead of a software app.

RSA used to be a leader in this area, but I see new players like Yubico replacing RSA keys with the modern Yubikey that incorporates several technologies (like OTP, WebAuthn) into one single key.

With the wide adoption of smartphones, I am not sure how widely hardware tokens would be adopted. But if you really like to have a backup option (in case you loose your phone) this would be a great alternative.

As you create, use several online accounts, I recommend you to choose the options that minimize sharing your personal information and at the same time keep your account secure.

Also be sure to setup two factor authentication to prevent/minimize your account from getting hacked.

Check this site to see what kind of options your website/app provides https://twofactorauth.org/

I hope that helps!
